# Multiplier 1: Mentoring & Coaching Leadership

This is **the hidden criterion** for promotion from Senior (L5) to Staff (L6+) in GAFAM. Without it, you stay a strong individual contributor forever.

## When to Mentor

**Identify mentoring candidates:**

- Junior or mid-level engineers showing potential but not yet autonomous
- Someone who asks you the same question 3+ times (sign: they need coaching, not answers)
- Engineers you're doing code review on frequently (teaching opportunity)
- People who will likely outlast you in the organization (invest early)

**What mentoring is NOT:**

- Answering Slack questions (that's help, not mentoring)
- Writing code for them (that's babysitting)
- Telling them the answer (that's teaching, not developing)

**What mentoring IS:**

- Asking questions: "What would you do here? Why? What could go wrong?"
- Structuring their growth: "Let's work on architectural thinking over the next quarter"
- Advocating for their opportunities: Promoting them in reviews, assigning stretch projects
- Holding them accountable: "You said you'd learn distributed tracing — show me what you found"

## Mentoring Structure (The Real Pattern)

**Step 1: Goal-setting (First 1:1)**

- Ask: "Where do you want to be in 2 years?"
- Listen. Don't assume.
- Turn aspiration into 3-5 concrete skills or achievements
- Example: "I want senior level" -> "Let's work on: system design thinking, leadership in chaotic situations, mentoring others, writing design docs"

**Step 2: Cadence (Ongoing)**

- **Scheduled 1:1s** (bi-weekly minimum, 30-45 min)
- **Project assignment** (give them stretch work aligned to goals)
- **Feedback cycles** (after each project, specific growth feedback)
- **Checking in** ("How's progression on architectural thinking? What blockers?")

**Step 3: Active Development (Each 1:1)**

- Do NOT: "You're doing great, keep going"
- Do: Ask them to **bring a problem**
  - "Here's a system I need to redesign. Walk me through your approach."
  - Listen. Ask clarifying questions.
  - Point out gaps (but don't solve it).
  - Next week: "How did you approach it? Show me the design doc."

**Step 4: Advocacy (Quarterly calibrations)**

- Write their promotion packet (or inputs)
- Pitch their readiness: "They've grown from mid to senior because of X, Y, Z"
- Push back on unfair criticism; champion their wins
- This is where most mentors fail — they develop people but don't advocate

## Measuring Mentoring Success

**Red flags (you're not actually mentoring):**

- You answer every question, they never try first
- They've made zero architectural decisions
- 6+ months have passed, they're not noticeably more confident
- When you're sick/on leave, they panic (you're the crutch, not the coach)

**Green flags (it's working):**

- They start asking smarter questions (not just "what?", now "why?", "what if?")
- They push back on your ideas sometimes (healthy disagreement)
- They tackle ambiguous problems without waiting for spec
- Their code review comments sound like yours (maturation, not copycat)
- In 12-24 months, they reach next level (mid -> senior, or senior -> staff)

**The ultimate metric:** They're mentoring someone else.

## Common Mentoring Failure Modes

| Failure | What's Really Happening | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| "I don't have time to mentor" | Too busy executing, not multiplying | Set 2h/week boundary; it's not optional at senior |
| "I'm not good at explaining" | You're solving instead of asking | Practice: next time, ask 3 questions before giving answer |
| "They don't seem interested" | You picked wrong person or approach | Ask them directly: "Do you want to grow? In what direction?" Adjust or find different mentee |
| "I've been mentoring them 18 months, no progress" | Mentee not committed or you're too soft | Be honest: "This isn't working." Suggest different mentor or reset expectations |
