# Mom Test — bank of validation questions

Primary source: Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test* (2013). https://www.momtestbook.com/

Curriculum: Harvard, UCL, Seedcamp, Microsoft Ventures, Shopify.

---

## The 3 non-negotiable rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.**
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.**
3. **Talk less and listen more.**

> "People lie because they want to be polite and supportive, especially when they see how passionate you are."

---

## ❌ Questions to NEVER ask

- "Don't you think it'd be cool to have [X]?"
- "Would you use [feature] if it existed?"
- "How much would you pay for [product]?"
- "What do you think of my idea?"
- "Would you like a tool that [...]?"
- "Imagine you had [X], how would you use it?"

→ All of these questions ask for an **opinion about the future** or a **polite validation**. The answers are useless.

---

## ✅ Effective Mom Test questions (by theme)

### Uncover a real problem

- "Tell me about the last time you had to [task]. How did it go?"
- "What was the most painful part?"
- "What have you tried to solve it?"
- "Why didn't that solution work?"
- "How long did it take you?"
- "How often does this happen to you?"

### Measure the pain (= measure the commitment)

- "How much have you spent so far to solve this problem?"
- "Who on your team is responsible for this?"
- "What tools are you using today?"
- "What percentage of your weekly time does this take?"

### Map the workflow

- "Show me — open the screen you use for this."
- "Which step takes you the most time?"
- "When you're stuck on [X], who do you call?"

### Test for real commitment (strong signal)

- "If I deliver a basic version tomorrow, can you test it with me on [a real case]?"
- "Can you introduce me to [another similar person]?"
- "Would you be OK to block 30 minutes on Monday for feedback?"
- "Here's a mockup — could you use it in your current workflow?"

→ An answer like "yes, I'll test it tomorrow with X" = **commitment**. A vague answer = a negative signal.

---

## The 2 outputs that matter

According to Fitzpatrick, count as positive only:

1. **Commitment** — they sacrifice something they value: time (a meeting blocked out), money (a deposit, a pre-order), reputation (an intro to a peer, a public tweet).
2. **Advancement** — they take the next step in the funnel: from call to demo, from demo to pilot, from pilot to contract.

Everything else (compliments, "that's cool", "interesting") = **noise**.

## Capture format (template)

For each interview, capture:

```
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Interviewee: [name, role, context]
Duration: [min]

## Verbatim quotes (not paraphrase)
- "..."
- "..."

## Task/context reported (past, not opinion)
- ...

## Current workflow & tools
- ...

## Pain expressed (measured in time/money/frequency)
- ...

## Commitment signal
- [none | time | money | reputation | intro]

## Advancement signal
- [none | next meeting | demo | pilot | contract]

## Inference (to validate, not a fact)
- ...
```

5 interviews in this format = the minimum data to move from Phase 1 → 2.

## Common anti-patterns

| Symptom | Cause | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| The interviewee says "great!" but commits to nothing | Politeness, not signal | Ask for a concrete commitment before wrapping up |
| You talk 60% of the time | You're pitching instead of listening | Reframe: "tell me instead about..." |
| You ask hypothetical questions | Rule 2 violated | Rephrase as "the last time you..." |
| You don't note the exact quotes | Memory is too optimistic | Record (with consent) or type live |
| You pick interviewees from your immediate network | Sympathy bias | Widen it: cold LinkedIn, Reddit, industry communities |
