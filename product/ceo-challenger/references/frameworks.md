# Reference frameworks & research

This file distills the empirical sources and frameworks the CEO Challenger can cite when precision is needed. **Don't load this file on every invocation** — SKILL.md is enough for 90% of decisions. Load it only when:

- The user disputes a recommendation and wants the empirical grounding
- The user asks "where does this framework come from?"
- An exact quote is needed to back up a point
- The user wants to go deeper and asks for the resources

## Phase 1 — The 4 major empirical studies on what sets a great CEO apart

### 1. CEO Genome Project (ghSMART — Botelho, Powell, Kincaid, Wang)

**Primary source:** Elena Lytkina Botelho et al., *"What Sets Successful CEOs Apart"*, Harvard Business Review, May-June 2017.

**Methodology:** A database of 17,000+ C-suite assessments, including 2,000 CEOs, compiled by ghSMART (a leadership advisory firm).

**Key finding:** Charisma, confidence, and pedigree have **little correlation** with CEO success. Boards often recruit on those traits — statistically, that's a mistake. Introverts are slightly more likely to exceed expectations.

**The 4 behaviors that set top performers apart:**

1. **Decisiveness** — They decide fast, recognizing that an imperfect decision beats indecision. *"A wrong decision is often better than no decision."*
2. **Engage for Impact** — They understand stakeholders' priorities and align them around a value-creation objective.
3. **Adapt Proactively** — They spend more time thinking about the long term and adjust before the signals become glaring.
4. **Deliver Reliably** — They do what they said they'd do, consistently. Reliability beats talent.

**Derived book:** Botelho, Powell, Kincaid, Wang, *The CEO Next Door* (Currency, 2018).

**Usable key quote:** *"Charisma, confidence, and pedigree all have little bearing on CEO success."*

### 2. CEO Excellence (McKinsey — Dewar, Keller, Malhotra)

**Primary source:** Carolyn Dewar, Scott Keller, Vikram Malhotra, *CEO Excellence: The Six Mindsets That Distinguish the Best Leaders from the Rest* (Scribner, 2022).

**Methodology:** A study of ~8,000 CEOs globally, with 67 in-depth interviews of top CEOs including Satya Nadella (Microsoft), Mary Barra (GM), Ken Chenault (American Express), Jamie Dimon (JPMorgan Chase).

**The CEO's 6 primary responsibilities:**

1. **Setting Direction** — define the vision, the strategy, and the explicit trade-offs
2. **Aligning the Organization** — culture, talent, organizational architecture
3. **Mobilizing Through Leaders** — the composition and effectiveness of the leadership team
4. **Engaging the Board** — strategic partnership, not just reporting
5. **Connecting with Stakeholders** — investors, customers, regulators, society
6. **Managing Personal Effectiveness** — time, energy, personal development

**Key finding:** excellent CEOs operate across all 6 dimensions *simultaneously* and act as integrators. None is optional.

**Usable key quote:** *"To be at your best and be effective as a leader, it's critical that you have moments and periods of recovery and resilience."*

### 3. Good to Great / Level 5 Leadership (Jim Collins)

**Primary source:** Jim Collins, *"Level 5 Leadership: The Triumph of Humility and Fierce Resolve"*, Harvard Business Review, Jan 2001. Developed in *Good to Great* (HarperBusiness, 2001).

**Methodology:** Analysis of 1,435 companies over 40+ years. 11 identified as "great" (defined by 3 extraordinary jumps in stock-market returns over 15 years, independent of industry).

**Key finding:** Every company that became "great" had a **Level 5 leader** at the moment of transition. None of the comparison companies did. The result was so consistent that it's described as "empirical, not ideological".

**Level 5 Leadership = humility + fierce resolve** (a combination, not a compromise):

- **Personal humility** — ambition for the cause, not for oneself. They take responsibility for failures first, and attribute successes to luck or to the team.
- **Fierce resolve** — an indomitable will to execute and do whatever it takes for the company to succeed, including the uncomfortable decisions.

**Key principle: "First Who, Then What"** — get the right people on the bus before deciding where it's going. Strategy flows from the team, not the other way around.

**Usable key quote:** *"Level 5 leaders are a study in duality: modest and willful, humble and fearless."*

### 4. The Outsiders — Capital Allocation (William Thorndike)

**Primary source:** William N. Thorndike Jr., *The Outsiders: Eight Unconventional CEOs and Their Radically Rational Blueprint for Success* (Harvard Business Review Press, 2012).

**Methodology:** A study of 8 unconventional CEOs: Tom Murphy (Capital Cities), Henry Singleton (Teledyne), Bill Anders (General Dynamics), John Malone (TCI), Katharine Graham (Washington Post), Bill Stiritz (Ralston Purina), Dick Smith (General Cinema), Warren Buffett (Berkshire Hathaway).

**Key finding:** These 8 CEOs produced returns **20× the S&P 500** over 25 years on average — a gap not explained by sector or timing.

**Central thesis:** *"The CEO's most crucial role is not operational prowess, but capital allocation."* The CEO's #1 role isn't operations — it's the rational allocation of capital.

**The 5 options for deploying capital:**

1. Invest in existing operations
2. Acquire other companies
3. Issue dividends
4. Pay down debt
5. Buy back shares (buybacks)

**The 3 options for raising capital:**

1. Internal cash flow
2. Debt
3. Equity

**Common traits of the Outsiders:**

- Laser focus on value per share (not on earnings or revenue growth)
- Belief that cash flow, not accounting earnings, determines long-term value
- Radical empirical rationality (rejecting Wall Street orthodoxy)
- Humble, frugal, avoiding the press and management fads
- Making counterintuitive decisions when the numbers justify them

**Endorsement:** Recommended #1 by Warren Buffett in the 2012 Berkshire Hathaway annual letter.

**Usable key quote:** *"Capital allocation is the CEO's most important job."* — Thorndike's adaptation of the Buffett thesis.

## Phase 2 — The operational frameworks for excelling in each dimension

### Decisiveness — Bezos Type 1 / Type 2 Decisions

**Primary source:** Jeff Bezos, Amazon Shareholder Letter, 2015.

**Framework:**

- **Type 1 (one-way doors)** — irreversible decisions, or ones costly to reverse. Must be made *"methodically, carefully, slowly, with great deliberation and consultation"*.
- **Type 2 (two-way doors)** — reversible decisions. Must be made *"quickly, by high judgment individuals or small groups"*. Experiment and iterate.

**The 70% rule:** Bezos considers 70% of the necessary information to be the right cut-off for deciding. Waiting for 90% is often too late. *"Being wrong may be less costly than you think, whereas being slow is going to be expensive for sure."*

**The problem with large organizations:** they treat every problem as Type 1 → paralysis, risk aversion, loss of experimentation. It's the default failure mode of scale.

**Usable key quote:** *"Most decisions should probably be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you had. If you wait for 90%, in most cases, you're probably being slow."*

### Operational Excellence — Andy Grove, High Output Management

**Primary source:** Andrew S. Grove (former CEO of Intel), *High Output Management* (Random House, 1983, still canonical).

**Key principles:**

1. **Everything is a repeatable production process** — understand the inputs, outputs, timing, limiting steps, quality controls
2. **Detect and fix problems at the earliest possible stage** (the least costly)
3. **Measure with few but vital indicators** — leading + trending, not lagging
4. **Management by Objectives (MBO)** — 2 questions: *"Where do I want to go?"* (the objective) + *"How will I pace myself to see if I'm getting there?"* (the key results). The ancestor of OKRs.
5. **A manager's output** = their team's output + the output of the neighboring teams they influence. Not what they produce themselves.

**Usable key quote:** *"A manager's output is the output of the organizations under his or her supervision or influence."*

### People & Hiring — Geoff Smart, Who: The A Method

**Primary source:** Geoff Smart & Randy Street, *Who: The A Method for Hiring* (Ballantine Books, 2008). Geoff is the founder of ghSMART, the same firm behind the CEO Genome Project.

**Finding:** A documented 90% success rate with the method. Based on interviews with 20 billionaires + 300 CEOs. The Economist calls bad hiring the *"single biggest problem in business today"*, with an average error cost of **$1.5M+ per year** for a senior role.

**The method in 4 steps:**

1. **Scorecard** — before opening a role, write a blueprint: why the job exists, what success looks like (quantified outcomes), the key competencies, the cultural fit. This is an *outcome-based job description*, not a list of responsibilities.
2. **Source** — constantly, not only when a need arises. Keep a talent pipeline open at all times.
3. **Select** — structured topgrading interviews focused on real past outcomes (not hypothetical future ones). Multiple rounds, multiple evaluators.
4. **Sell** — once the A-player is identified, sell the role actively. A-players always have options.

**Usable key quote:** *"The average hiring mistake costs 15 times an employee's base salary in hard costs and productivity loss."*

### Self-Management — Peter Drucker, The Effective Executive

**Primary source:** Peter F. Drucker, *The Effective Executive: The Definitive Guide to Getting the Right Things Done* (Harper & Row, 1966). Still canonical 60 years on.

**The 5 practices of an effective executive:**

1. **Know where your time goes** — a 2-week time log to see the reality, not the perception. *"Effective executives start by finding out where their time actually goes."*
2. **Focus on contribution** — ask *"What results are expected of me?"* before *"What work to do?"*. Outward focus on results, not inward focus on tasks.
3. **Build on strengths** — your own, other people's, and the situation's. Never build on weaknesses. Weaknesses aren't worth fixing.
4. **Setting priorities** — *"Do first things first, and second things not at all."* Concentration is the counterpart of prioritization: it's the courage to say no to the rest.
5. **Effective decisions** — based on dissenting opinions, not on consensus. *"The effective decision-maker does not start with the assumption that one proposed course of action is right and that all others must be wrong."*

**Usable key quote:** *"Time is the scarcest resource, and unless it is managed, nothing else can be managed."*

### Stakeholder Management — Trillion Dollar Coach (Bill Campbell)

**Primary source:** Eric Schmidt, Jonathan Rosenberg, Alan Eagle, *Trillion Dollar Coach: The Leadership Playbook of Silicon Valley's Bill Campbell* (Harper Business, 2019). Based on 80+ interviews.

**Context:** Bill Campbell (1940-2016), a former football coach turned mentor to the biggest names: Steve Jobs (Apple), Larry Page (Google), Eric Schmidt (Google), Jeff Bezos (Amazon). Credited with helping create more than $1 trillion of market value.

**Key principles:**

- *"Your title makes you a manager, your people make you a leader. To be a good leader you need to first be an excellent manager."*
- **Trust before task** — build trust first, results follow.
- **Complete candor** — tell the truth with care, not with brutality. No political hedging.
- **Believe in people more than they believe in themselves** — literally raise individual ambitions.
- *"The most important thing a manager does is to help people be more effective, and to grow and develop."*
- **Ritualized 1-on-1s** — fixed cadence, agenda owned by the report, the manager listens 70% of the time.

### Burnout & Personal Effectiveness — McKinsey Health Institute

**Primary source:** McKinsey Health Institute, *"Addressing employee burnout: Are you solving the right problem?"*, 2022-2023. A study across 15 countries.

**Key finding:** *"Toxic workplace behavior predicts more than 60% of the variance in burnout"* — far ahead of every other organizational variable combined.

**Implication for the CEO:** individual interventions (yoga, meditation, wellness apps) don't move the needle if the organizational behaviors aren't addressed. The CEO is both the potential cause of their teams' burnout **and** the primary target of their own. Auditing one's own contribution to toxicity is a critical CEO exercise.

**Usable key quote:** *"Employees who report experiencing high levels of toxic behavior at work are almost eight times more likely than those who don't to experience burnout symptoms."*

## How to use these frameworks in the challenge

**Golden rule:** one relevant framework > five frameworks in a list. The choice of which framework to cite is itself an editorial decision that demonstrates competence.

**Matching situation → framework:**

| User's situation | Framework to cite |
|---|---|
| "I can't decide" | Bezos Type 1/2 + the 70% rule |
| "How much time on this feature?" | Grove limiting steps + indicators |
| "Should I hire someone?" | Geoff Smart scorecard first |
| "I'm afraid of getting it wrong" | CEO Genome decisiveness + Bezos 70% |
| "I'm overwhelmed, I can't prioritize anymore" | Drucker's 5 practices + time log |
| "Is this idea any good?" | Thorndike's 5 deployment options |
| "How do I convince my board / partner?" | Trillion Dollar Coach candor |
| "I'm thinking about pivoting" | CEO Excellence "setting direction" + Good to Great "level 5 humility" |
| "My team is exhausted" | McKinsey burnout + personal toxic-behavior audit |

**Tone rule:** when you cite a framework, do it in one sentence max — not a paragraph. The framework is a sword, not a lecture.

**Example:**
- ❌ *"According to the CEO Genome Project, a study conducted by ghSMART across 17,000 assessments including 2,000 CEOs, published in Harvard Business Review in 2017 by Botelho et al., decisiveness is one of the 4 behaviors that set top performers apart from the rest, alongside engage for impact, adapt proactively, and deliver reliably..."*
- ✅ *"Bezos calls it the 70% rule: decide at 70% of the info, don't wait for 90%. You're at 85% and still waiting — that's perfectionism disguised as diligence."*
